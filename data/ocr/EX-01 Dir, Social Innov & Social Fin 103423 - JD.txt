NATURE AND SCOPE The mission of Employment and Social Development Canada (ESDC) is to build a stronger and more competitive Canada, to support Canadians in making choices that enable them to live productive and rewarding lives and to improve the quality of life of Canadians. This is accomplished through the development of policies to promote a society in which all Canadians are able to use their talents, skills and resources to participate in leamming, work and their communities. ESDC also creates programs and supports initiatives to enable Canadians to move through life s transitions, and create enhanced outcomes for Canadians, through service excellence with Service Canada and its other partners. ESDC is mandated to provide a leadership role and focal point for federal social, labour market and economic programs and activities related to the development of Canada s human capital. The ESDC department, through Service Canada, supports Canadians and their communities by providing citizen-centered, quality service through a network of people, partnerships and technology. This enables Canadians to access the full range of Government services and benefits by providing seamless, comprehensive one-stop service to more Canadians in more communities, and delivering services in a manner that ensures a positive service experience for Canadians. The ESDC Income Security and Social Development Branch is mandated to develop and deliver innovative and forward looking social policies and programs to ensure that children, families, seniors, people with disabilities, homeless individuals and families as well as those at risk of being homeless, communities and others who are facing social and economic challenges have the support, knowledge and information they need to maintain their well-being and facilitate their Participation in society. Within the Branch, the Social Innovation and Community Development Directorate directs the development and implementation of national programs and initiatives, including the Social Development Partnership Program to assist citizens, communities, the not-for-profit sector, private sector and other stakeholders to implement their own solutions to social and economic issues. The Directorate actively engages and collaborates on initiatives across government and with external partners; develops national policies and partnerships; manages knowledge transfer, and formulates solutions to complex socio-economic challenges on a cross-sectoral basis. Two new approaches that the Branch/Directorate is pursuing involves Social Innovation and Social Finance. Social innovation is about developing new solutions to social or economic challenges, which can improve people s quality of life through collaborating with new partners, testing creative 2
ideas and measuring their impact. Social innovation often involves collaboration across different levels of government, charities, the not-for-profit and private sectors to take action on a common social issue. By innovating socially, community organizations can find new solutions to enduring problems and share them with others across the country and around the world. Social finance is a tool that seeks to mobilize private capital for the public good. It creates opportunities for investors to finance projects that benefit society and for community organizations to access new sources of funds. Within this context the Director, Social Innovation and Social Finance (SISF) is accountable for program policy design, implementation and the provision of advice to senior management and departmental officials relating to the two comprehensive initiatives - Social Innovation and Social Finance. This is accomplished in collaboration with the Director, Social Policy, internal and extemal subject-matter experts, key partners and stakeholders. The Director is responsible for initiating and leading research, studies, pilots, workshops, conferences and activities to generate and diffuse knowledge, engage and mobilize stakeholders, identify and analyze socio-economic local, regional, national and international trends and issues, in order to inform the development and the implementation of program policies relating to social innovation and social finance. Social finance investments involve access to capital that has a positive impact not only financially but also on society as a whole, and can be made by different types of investors. These include 3
charitable foundations, retail investors, credit unions, chartered banks, governments and institutional investors such as pension funds, and can be made to a variely of organizations that seek to have a positive impact in their communities. These include registered charities, non-profit organizations, co- operatives and businesses that consider the social effects of their activities; can be made using a variety of financial tools, including loans, community bonds, equity investments and social impact bonds; and can help non-profit organizations and charities become more successful. Though it does not replace traditional government funding, social finance can help organizations attract new financial resources to help them address social issues.
